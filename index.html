Scientific Scope

About the Workshop

The workshop will provide a systems-level view of safety-critical robotics. Rather than focusing on one narrow sub-topic, it will connect robot embodiment, modeling, planning, control, and learning under the common objective of safe operation in industrial and human-centered environments.

A particular emphasis will be placed on safety challenges in real-world deployment, including manufacturing systems, logistics automation, and human-robot collaboration. The scientific scope reflects the state of the art in robotics and automation by bringing together classical safety mechanisms and modern autonomy.

Topics include robot design with intrinsic compliance; dynamic modeling for safe interaction; motion planning and navigation under safety constraints; reactive and barrier-based safety control; human-robot collaboration; safety-aware learning; robustness under uncertainty; and fail-safe or recovery behaviors.

The workshop also differentiates itself from prior related events. Over the last several years, workshops at ICRA, IROS, RSS, CoRL, and CASE have addressed individual aspects of safety, such as formal methods, robot safety under uncertainty, safe and robust robot learning, foundation-model safety, and safer autonomous navigation. In contrast, this workshop emphasizes a cross-layer perspective on safety-critical robotics and explicitly bridges model-based and learning-based approaches for deployable autonomy.

Invited Talks

Keynote Speakers

Talk 1

Heye Huang

Korea Advanced Institute of Science & Technology (KAIST)

Safe embodied AI and autonomous driving

Talk details

Autonomous systems operating in real-world open environments face a persistent long-tail safety challenge: rare but high-risk scenarios are difficult to anticipate, underrepresented in standard datasets, and often require reasoning beyond pattern recognition. This talk rethinks safe autonomy from the perspective of rare risks and reliable decisions. It presents a unified framework that connects long-tail high-risk scenario data, prior risk knowledge, multimodal semantic understanding, and large language model reasoning to support risk-aware autonomous decision-making in complex traffic environments. By constructing knowledge-augmented risk representations and integrating them with LLM-based reasoning, the framework enables autonomous systems to assess risk sources, understand interaction context, and generate interpretable decisions in rare, extreme, and interaction-intensive scenarios. Ultimately, this work aims to provide new insights into how high-risk data and generative AI can jointly support trustworthy autonomous driving in complex open environments.

Talk 2

Liang Hu

Harbin Institute of Technology, Shenzhen

Robot localization and navigation

Talk details

Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) provide a principled framework for enforcing safety in robotic systems. However, safety requirements may conflict with task performance and input limitations, causing infeasibility or conservative behavior, while learned safety knowledge is often tied to a specific task. This talk presents a coherent progress in CBF-based safe control, navigation, and learning. A safety-first hierarchical quadratic programming framework is introduced to guarantee feasibility while prioritizing safety under multiple constraints, enabling reliable operation in complex environments. This principle is then extended to reinforcement learning through a certificated actor-critic method, where CBF-derived critics assess safety and guide policy improvement. Finally, transferable model-free safety filters are developed to learn perception-based CBFs from a single policy and reuse the acquired safety knowledge across different robotic tasks. Together, these works demonstrate how CBFs can support safe control, reliable navigation, and generalizable safety learning.

Talk 3

Wei Pan

Newcastle University

Machine learning for robotics

Talk details

If robots are going to work outside the lab, the models they rely on to predict and control have to be trustworthy: reliable enough that a failure doesn't hurt anyone, cheap enough to train, general enough to reuse across different robots, and able to run alongside the hardware and controllers people already have. I'll argue that a model earns that trust by building in the physics we already know rather than hoping it emerges from data, and by putting honest bounds on whatever it can't capture.

The talk moves through three stages: modelling, control, and safety. Modelling starts with Bayesian system identification for nonlinear and switched systems, and diffusion models for the residual forces that simpler models leave out. Control covers adaptive schemes that keep working when the dynamics change suddenly, and a Koopman method that lets one policy transfer across many robot configurations on a fraction of the training data previous methods need. Safety brings in world models built for real deployment, including a vehicle-dynamics-embedded model and a decoder-free one, together with conformal prediction as a way to attach honest confidence bounds to what the model predicts. A single idea connects the three stages: model the part of the physics you know, and deal explicitly with the part you don't, whether that's the residual forces a controller must absorb or the uncertainty a world model must bound. Results run across drones, manipulators, quadrupeds, and vehicles, and return to the same rules throughout: build the physics in, don't cut corners on reliability, make every real trial count, and work with the systems already in place.

Talk 4

Yanlong Huang

University of Leeds

Robot learning

Talk details

In the community of robot learning, learning from demonstrations (LfD) has been studied widely due to its convenient solution for transferring human skills to robots. A myriad of applications of LfD were reported in previous literature, e.g., robot manipulation and human-robot collaboration. In this talk, I will discuss a nonparametric imitation learning framework kernelized movement primitives (KMP), which can address several crucial issues such as learning multiple demonstrations, reproduction and adaptation, and learning trajectories associated with high-dimensional inputs. Later, I will show some extensions of KMP, including the learning of robot orientations and stiffness matrices, uncertainty-aware human-robot interactions, stable and constrained learning, as well as fast adaptation and closed-loop learning for KMP.

Talk 5

Matteo Saveriano

University of Trento

Human-robot interaction

Talk details

Robotic manipulation requires sophisticated control policies and precise regulation of the forces exchanged with the environment. While learning-based methods have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in acquiring complex manipulation skills, they still lack formal guarantees of safety and stability during both learning and execution. In this talk, I will present recent advances in stability- and passivity-based learning and control, and illustrate their application to contact-rich robotic manipulation and learning on manifold-valued data.

Talk 6

Zhao Guo

Wuhan University

Actuator design and human-robot interaction

Talk details

The current rehabilitation robot still lacks accurate diagnosis and personalized rehabilitation strategies, resulting in poor rehabilitation training effects and difficulties in application. This report introduces the research and exploration of our team in rehabilitation exoskeleton robot, including the design of lower limb exoskeleton robots, multimodal perception and intelligent evaluation of human motion intention, and research on robot personalized rehabilitation and adaptive control technology to solve the key technical bottlenecks in the clinical application of lower limb rehabilitation robots.

Talk 7

Ning Li

Shenyang Institute of Automation, Chinese Academy of Sciences

Rehabilitation and assistive robotics

Talk details

Human-like robotic systems, such as humanoid robots and exoskeletons, represent an important form of extending and augmenting human motor capabilities. They are gradually moving beyond human–robot interaction and assistive applications toward broader scenarios in civil services, industrial operations, medical rehabilitation, and military applications, with the potential to become the “next organ” of the human body. However, although these systems are structurally and functionally related to the human body, most existing studies still follow conventional robotic design paradigms. They have not yet established actuation, perception, and control mechanisms that are deeply matched with the human neuromuscular system, making it difficult to achieve natural, efficient, and compliant human–robot coordination. This talk will focus on robotic systems based on life-like mechanisms, with an emphasis on recent progress in human-like robotic systems inspired by population-distributed neuromuscular motor mechanisms. From a cross-scale perspective spanning both macro- and micro-level motion principles, the talk will introduce the modeling of muscle-fiber-like actuation, the design of human-compatible artificial muscle actuation units and their clustered architectures, and human–robot collaborative control methods based on population recruitment effects. These studies preliminarily reveal the intrinsic mechanism by which the human motor system achieves demand-driven activation and precise regulation, while also exploring a technical pathway for human-like robotic systems to evolve from “mechanical assistive devices” toward “bio-integrated systems.” This research represents an interdisciplinary effort at the intersection of human biomechanics, neuromuscular control, and robotics. It is expected to provide theoretical support and key technical foundations for the future transition of human-like robotic systems from external mechanical assistance to embodied integration with the human body.
